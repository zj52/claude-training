The framework

The 3-part prompt.

Anthropic’s three-part frame is the quickest way to lift the quality of any prompt you write. Set the stage with context, give Claude the task, and specify what you want the output to look like.

1

Set the stage

Who you are, what you’re doing, what success looks like. Attach reference files if useful.

2

Give the task

One clear, specific ask. Break compound tasks into numbered steps.

3

Specify the output

Format, length, audience, tone. Paste an example if you have one.

Prompt library

Starter prompts, by task.

Award narrative

Draft a justification paragraph

Turn a program description plus existing proposal into a tight 150-word justification.

I'm drafting a justification for an NSF supplement. Below is the program
description and the original proposal abstract.

[Paste program description]
[Paste proposal abstract]

Write a 150-word case for the supplement that:
- Connects the supplement scope directly to the program priorities
- Uses plain professional English (no marketing language)
- Ends with one sentence on broader impact

Match the voice and specificity of the abstract.
Policy summary

One-paragraph summary for a non-expert

Read a long policy document and produce a plain-English summary you can send to a PI.

I'm sharing this policy document with a PI who has no background in
research administration. Read the attached document and produce:

1. A single paragraph (under 100 words) that explains what the policy
   says in plain English.
2. A short bulleted list of any actions the PI needs to take.
3. A one-line subject line for the email I'll send.

Tone: collegial, not legalistic.

[Attach: policy.pdf]
Meeting prep

Brief me on a topic before a meeting

Use this when you need a focused briefing on a specific topic ahead of a meeting.

I have a meeting in 30 minutes about [topic]. Brief me with:

1. A 3-sentence overview of where things stand.
2. The top 3 questions I should be ready to answer.
3. The top 2 questions I should ask.
4. Any obvious risks or open issues.

Keep the whole brief under 200 words. Skip generic advice — assume I
know the basics. Focus on what's specific to [topic].
