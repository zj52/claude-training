## What you'll learn

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

- Use the **Delegation–Diligence loop** to systematically validate AI's analytical capabilities for your specific work.
- Apply **Description and Discernment** to identify patterns in your data while recognizing AI's limitations.
- Build confidence in AI-assisted analysis by **testing against data you already understand.**

## Data analysis with AI (video, 7 min)

The video addresses a critical question: *how do I know I can trust the results?* We follow Rio, a program director at Valley Veterans Services, as he uses the Delegation–Diligence loop to systematically validate AI's analytical capabilities using past data where he already knows the answers. The video demonstrates how to build confidence through testing, identify capability gaps, and develop an approach you can apply to new data with confidence.

## Key takeaways

- **Test AI against data you already understand.** Before trusting AI with new analysis, validate it using past data where you know the correct results. If AI can match known results with the right guidance, you can confidently use it for similar future tasks.
- **Use Discernment to identify gaps in AI's reasoning.** As you test, note where AI misses important context and what additional Description you need to provide.
- **Build validated approaches, not blind trust.** Each testing round teaches you what AI does well and where it needs guidance. Document what works so you can replicate it.
- **AI can help even if you're not data-savvy.** AI can brainstorm solutions, write Excel formulas, and reformat messy data — just keep asking for clarifications so you understand the process.
- **Validation builds confidence but doesn't eliminate responsibility.** You're still accountable for checking that results make sense and being transparent about AI's role.

## Exercise 1 — Messaging analysis

Uses lower-stakes data (your own public communications) to practice the Description–Discernment loop for data analysis.

### Part I: Gather your data

Collect 10–20 examples of your organization's communications — social media posts, email subject lines, newsletter headlines, or event announcements. Include a mix of high-performing and lower-performing content.

### Part II: Analyze with AI

Share your dataset and ask AI to identify patterns:

- What themes or topics appear in your higher-performing content?
- What language, tone, or formatting patterns emerge?
- Are there any gaps between what you communicate and what resonates?

### Part III: Apply Discernment

- Do the identified patterns match your intuition about what works?
- What context is AI missing about your audience or goals?
- Are there patterns AI identified that surprise you?

### Reflection

- What are you trying to learn from your dataset?
- How does higher-performing content align with your authentic voice and organizational values?
- Are you reaching the right audience?

**Stretch goal:** Use AI to audit how your messaging compares with your organization's stated mission and values, find discrepancies, and create a messaging guide from the analysis.

## Exercise 2 — Analyzing donor giving patterns

Applies data analysis skills to high-stakes fundraising data, building on the data hygiene practices from Lesson 5.

### Part I: Prepare your data

Use the sanitized donor dataset from Lesson 5, or prepare a new one by removing PII. Ensure you have historical giving data across multiple time periods.

### Part II: Analyze with AI

Ask AI to identify patterns in:

- Donor retention rates over time
- Recurring vs. one-time donation patterns
- Campaign effectiveness comparisons
- Giving trends by amount ranges

### Part III: Apply Discernment

- Do the trends match what you know about your donor base?
- Is AI only focusing on monetary value, missing relationship factors?
- What patterns would help strengthen donor relationships, not just maximize revenue?

### Reflection

- What might the costs of implementing efficiency recommendations be? (E.g., if findings suggest focusing on major donors at the expense of small donors, what's the impact on community perception or long-term sustainability?)
- What patterns would help strengthen relationships with donors beyond just giving amounts?

## Exercise 3 — Trend analysis to anticipate community needs (stretch)

Combines multiple data sources to practice predictive analysis.

### Part I: Gather diverse sources

Collect information you already use to understand community needs:

- Your own program data and service requests
- External reports or datasets about your community
- News or policy developments affecting your constituents

### Part II: Analyze for emerging patterns

Ask AI to help you identify:

- Trends in the types of support people are requesting
- External factors that might increase or change demand
- Gaps between current services and emerging needs

### Part III: Apply rigorous Discernment

This analysis requires the highest level of critical evaluation:

- How do AI's predictions compare with your direct community experience?
- What systemic factors or local context might AI be missing?
- What values do you need to keep in mind as you anticipate community needs with dignity and respect?

### Reflection

- How can you approach this process responsibly?
- What factors and systemic issues can explain or contextualize what AI cannot?

## Lesson reflection

- How did testing AI against data you already understood change your confidence in using it for new analysis?
- What gaps or limitations did you identify that will shape how you delegate data analysis tasks in the future?

## Cornell adaptation notes

### The big idea: "test AI against data you already understand"

This is the most operationally useful principle in the entire course for research administrators. It's the practical answer to *"how do I deploy AI confidently in my actual work?"* and it maps directly onto **Deployment Diligence** from the 4D framework.

The Cornell version of this principle, for the workshop:

> **Before you let AI analyze a new dataset, run it on one you already know cold.** If AI can match what you already know — and you understand *why* it got there — you've earned the right to use it on the new one. If it can't, you've learned exactly where Description and Discernment need to be tighter.

### Cornell research-admin parallels for each exercise

- **Exercise 1 (messaging) → Sponsor announcement patterns.** Collect 10–20 past NIH program announcements (or NSF Dear Colleague Letters, or RFAs from your top sponsors). Ask AI to identify themes, language patterns, what gets emphasized. Validate against your own institutional read of those announcements. Builds skill at parsing future announcements quickly.
- **Exercise 2 (donor giving) → Sponsor award patterns.** Analyze Cornell's historical award data from a sponsor over multiple years: typical award sizes, study sections that fund Cornell, types of projects, success rates by department, time-to-award. The lesson's framing — *"is AI only focusing on monetary value, missing relationship factors?"* — translates directly: AI might miss program-officer relationships, institutional reputation factors, "soft" indicators of fit. Discernment matters most where the data is incomplete.
- **Exercise 3 (predictive trend analysis) → Forecasting sponsor priorities.** Combine federal budget reports, NIH/NSF strategic plans, agency press releases, and Cornell's own portfolio to anticipate where funding will move next year. *Highest stakes for Discernment* — Cornell's strategy decisions shouldn't rest on an AI hallucination about an NIH priority area. The lesson's "rigorous Discernment" guidance applies in full.

### Data hygiene caveat (from Lesson 5)

For Cornell research-admin examples, PI names, dollar amounts above certain thresholds, and proposal-stage info may need to be anonymized or held back depending on the sponsor / data classification. The general principle from Lesson 5 still applies: **work backwards from your goal to determine what data is truly necessary**. For trend analysis you usually need ranges, not specific dollar figures; categories, not specific PI identities.

### Module 2 design implication

This lesson is **the best candidate for a hands-on group exercise in Module 2.** Two reasons:

1. The "test on known data" principle is a habit that has to be *practiced* to stick. Reading it isn't enough.
2. Cornell research admins have abundant validation data — every past award, every closed proposal cycle, every previous fiscal year. They can build their own validation tests at their desk.

A suggested Module 2 mini-exercise: each participant brings one dataset they know well (an annual award report, a portfolio analysis, a past compliance dashboard), and we run a 10-minute validation test live with Claude 3P. The room watches the Diligence loop happen on real data.
