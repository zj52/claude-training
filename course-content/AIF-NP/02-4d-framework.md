## What you'll learn

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:

- Explain how the AI Fluency Framework (4Ds) can be applied to nonprofit work.
- Define each of the 4Ds — Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence.

## The 4D Framework (video, 6 min)

The video introduces the 4D Framework — four interconnected competencies that form the foundation of AI Fluency. It establishes two modes of interaction: the Delegation–Diligence loop for deciding *when and whether* to use AI, and the Description–Discernment loop for engaging with AI effectively day-to-day. Each competency is broken into three sub-components, with nonprofit-specific examples.

## Key takeaways

### The Delegation–Diligence loop — *when and whether* to use AI

- **Delegation** — deciding what work should be done by humans versus AI.
  - **Problem Awareness** — understanding your goals
  - **Platform Awareness** — knowing your tools' capabilities and limitations
  - **Task Delegation** — distributing work thoughtfully
- **Diligence** — taking responsibility for *how* you use AI.
  - **Creation Diligence** — being intentional about which tools you use
  - **Transparency Diligence** — being honest about AI's role
  - **Deployment Diligence** — verifying and vouching for outputs

### The Description–Discernment loop — *how* to use AI day-to-day

- **Description** — how you communicate effectively with AI systems.
  - **Product Description** — defining outputs
  - **Process Description** — guiding the approach
  - **Performance Description** — shaping AI behavior during collaboration
- **Discernment** — critically evaluating AI's work.
  - **Product Discernment** — evaluating quality
  - **Process Discernment** — evaluating how AI arrived at outputs
  - **Performance Discernment** — evaluating AI behavior

## Exercise 1 — Mapping your AI needs

**Why?** This exercise reveals which AI competencies you're already thinking about and which ones need attention, so you can strategically direct your energy where it matters most.

1. **Self-Reflection:** Write a list of your questions and concerns about using AI.
2. **Collaboration:** Open a conversation with AI. Ask AI: *"Can you help me understand where each of these questions and concerns fits within the AI Fluency 4D framework?"*
3. **Reflection:** Which of the 4 competencies did most of your questions and concerns fall into?
   - This pattern reveals where you're currently focusing your attention. Is this where you most need to develop, or are you avoiding a harder competency that would unlock more progress?
   - Based on the competency you identified to prioritize first, what's one specific thing you'll do differently the next time you use AI?

## Exercise 2 — Identify one cross-competency challenge (stretch)

**Stretch goal:** understand how each time you interact with AI you are (or should be) interacting with each of these competencies in an interconnected way.

After mapping your questions across the 4Ds, identify one area where you'll need to develop multiple competencies simultaneously to move forward effectively.

For example:
- *"I realize I need both Delegation skills (deciding if AI should help with volunteer communications) AND Diligence skills (ensuring we protect volunteer privacy) before I can move forward."*
- *"My Discernment concerns about accuracy won't matter until I develop Description skills to get better outputs in the first place."*

## Lesson reflection

- Looking at your mapped questions and concerns, were you surprised by which competency area dominated? What might that tell you about your current relationship with AI?
- How might the two loops (Delegation–Diligence and Description–Discernment) have changed a recent interaction you had with AI?

## Cornell adaptation notes

**This lesson is the conceptual spine of Module 2.** Everything in the rest of the course (and the rest of our workshop) is an application of these 12 sub-competencies (4 D's × 3 sub-components each).

### Mapping the 4Ds to research administration

- **Delegation** — *Should I use AI for this?*
  - Problem Awareness: am I clear on what I'm trying to accomplish? (e.g., "I need to summarize 2 CFR 200 for a PI" vs. "I need to figure out whether this clerical salary line is allowable")
  - Platform Awareness: does the tool I'm reaching for have the right access? (Claude 3P + eCFR MCP = right; Claude.ai with no tools = wrong tool for regulation-text work)
  - Task Delegation: which parts are AI-suited (synthesis, formatting, first-draft language) vs. human-only (the judgment call, the sponsor relationship, the sign-off)
- **Diligence** — *Am I taking responsibility?*
  - Creation Diligence: choosing approved Cornell tooling (Claude 3P), not random ChatGPT consumer free accounts that mix data
  - Transparency Diligence: when sending AI-assisted output to a PI, OSP colleague, or sponsor — is AI's role disclosed when it matters?
  - Deployment Diligence: every CFR citation, deadline, dollar figure verified before it leaves my hands
- **Description** — *How do I prompt well?*
  - Product Description: what kind of output ("a one-paragraph plain-English summary for a non-expert PI")
  - Process Description: how to approach it ("look up the actual reg, don't rely on memory, then summarize")
  - Performance Description: tone/role ("you are a sponsored programs analyst helping a busy PI")
  - **This maps directly onto our existing 3-part prompt teaching** on slides 8–9. Worth showing the alignment in Module 2: Cornell's "context · task · format" is a practical compression of AI Fluency's three Description sub-components.
- **Discernment** — *Is the output good?*
  - Product Discernment: is the answer correct, complete, useful?
  - Process Discernment: did AI use the right sources / reasoning / tools?
  - Performance Discernment: did AI handle the conversation appropriately (push back when uncertain, ask for clarification)?
